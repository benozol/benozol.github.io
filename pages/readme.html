README!

From time to time, I manage to read a book. Since I sometimes appreciate other people's opinion about books, here are mine.

Me

Richard Dawkins: The Blind Watchmaker

Dawkins puts total clarity into every little detail of his argument, very impressing. An outstanding exercise in presenting scientific ideas - the best book of popular science I've come across.

TC Boyle: World's End

Die Präzision im historischen und geographischen Hintergrund der Geschichte(n) ist beeindruckend. Vierhundert Jahre Sozialgeschichte in der US Ostküste. (dt. Übersetzung Richter)

Naomi Klein: Shock Doctrine

Klein's book implements in itself a kind of shock doctrine by depicting first in disturbing detail MK Ultra, a CIA torture program from the 1950ies (which went, maybe unrelatedly, since the publication of Shock Doctrine from total tinfoil-hat conspiracy theory to Wikipedia common sense). On this basis follows is a description of implementations of neoliberal programs (i.e., market fundamentalism, comprised of privatisation, market liberatisation, and austerity) in a number of countries, chapter-by-chapter. Her point of view is eye opening, and helps understanding other examples, like Greece, 2009.

Klein writing is impressively sharp, absolutely dangerous; for the good.

Richard Feinman: Surely you're Joking, Mr Feynman!

A highly entertaining list of good ideas by Feinman, a testimony of an impressively adaptable intelligence in doing interesting stuff, and his all too likeable blindness to social norms, almost without considering his works in physics, or mentioning his Nobel price.

Herman Melville: Moby Dick

Moby Dick is mostly known in its abridged version as an exciting adventure story about whaling and revenge (you know, for the lost leg). But this perspective misses two points in my opinion: The minor one is the immense detail about forgotten 19th century whaling technology – really all you ever wanted to know, including the countless ropes and tools used in the hunt and the pervasive role of whale oil in everyday life and industry. And that's already a fascinating, technological pinhole into the 19th century.

But more striking are the essayistic chapters that give hints on the interpretations beyond the story and the facts: ‘The Whiteness of the Whale’, ‘Of the Monstrous Pictures of Whales’, ‘Of Whales in Paint; …; in Stars’, ‘The Symphony’, ‘ The Honor and Glory of Whaling’. The whale seems to represent our nature – nature vis-à-vis technological advancement, and the last thread to human supremacy among living beings – and what was still left of a supreme being in these days. And whaling technology offers the tools to mankind to control our nature, and to dominate nature and the world.

The end of the story is of course quite drastic, and I am not sure how to take it (even if captain Ahab is quite blunt: ‘Talk not to me of blasphemy, man; I’d strike the sun if it insulted me.’). But a researcher, or anyone else who is participating in advancing and progressing knowledge and technology even with negligible achievements, should keep a little track of possible ends of these developments.

Astrid Lindgren: Ronja Räubertocher

Eine Geschichte über Räuber die in einer Ritterburg wohnen. Besser kann man wahrscheinlich das Interesse eines fünfjährigen nicht wecken. Zurecht. Auf der anderen Seite ist es ein völlig erwachsener Roman über Natur, Liebe, Leben, und den Tot; durchwegs wunderschön und liebevoll erzählt.

Wind in den Weiden

Wunderbare Freundschaft zwischen vier Tieren, voller gutherziger Charactere (außer dem Polizisten und dem Richter, klar), und traumhaften Ufer- und Waldlandschaften.
